In Long Island’s plans to tackle climate change, boosting tree cover is a good place to start.

June 20, 2023

By Karthika Namboothiri

You know your neighborhood could probably use more trees. Apart from the immediate benefits of a lush green canopy providing shade on a sweltering summer day, increasing urban tree cover equally across neighborhoods promises big gains.

Climate change experts have long predicted that temperatures are rising and local governments, including those in the state, are accounting for its effects. In New York, winters have warmed three times faster than summers, according to data from the Department of Conservation, bringing in less rain and snow. Islip in Suffolk County had its warmest summer last year and experienced its longest frost-free season. The number of heat waves per year is also expected to increase.

However, not everyone will be impacted the same way. A detailed analysis of satellite data from the U.S. Geological Survey of every census tract in Long Island shows that tree cover is disproportionately spread across the region. With almost as many inhabitants as Suffolk but less than a quarter in size, only 27 percent of Nassau County has tree cover compared to Suffolk’s average of 42 percent.

Let’s put that in context. If the area of tree cover was measured as Olympic size swimming pools, about six people in Nassau County would share one pool. Meanwhile every individual in Suffolk County could have their own.

In this post, tree cover refers to all kinds of trees; those in state and national parks, trees in private backyards, as well as those along sidewalks and parking lots.



Long Island’s most populous town of Hempstead has as little as 14 percent of tree cover, according to the analysis. In contrast, more than half of Shelter Island, where less than four thousand residents reside, is covered in trees.

Lower rates of tree cover in urban places can create “islands” of heat which causes concentrated areas of high temperature. The “urban heat island effect”, according to non-profit American Forests which advocates for increased tree equity, often disproportionately affects vulnerable populations, low-income communities and people of color.

In April, President Biden announced a 1 billion dollar grant to increase tree cover in urban spaces through the U.S. Forest Service and local communities. Numerous census tracts in both Nassau and Suffolk Counties have been identified by the program as disadvantaged, as per the Climate and Economic Justice Screening tool. The program is intended to tackle issues of “extreme heat, storm-induced flooding, and other climate impacts” through increasing urban forest cover.

It is estimated that more than a hundred thousand acres in Suffolk County can be used to plant more trees, according to The Nature Conservancy’s Reforestation Hub. Ensuring there is equitable access to green spaces across Long Island will be a promising step in its future.



Methodology

In order to calculate tree cover for Long island per census tract, I used LANDSAT satellite data from USGS’s National Land Cover Database. Using a method called zonal statistics, I calculated the average tree cover for each tract, city and town by overlaying boundary shapes. The total area of land, population as well as tree cover for Nassau, Suffolk and Long Island were calculated from this data and the shapefiles used to create the interactive map in Mapbox.
